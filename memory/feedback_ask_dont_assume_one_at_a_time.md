# If confused, ask. One thing at a time.

## Two rules combined

### Rule 1: Do not assume. Ask.

When information is incomplete, do not fabricate, infer, or apply a fact from one context to another to fill the gap. Ask Bill directly.

Recent violation (2026-04-14): Bill mentioned that GSC showed Spanish pages converting highest. I assumed that applied to BOTH GlobalHighLevel.com AND SafeBathGrabBar.com and wrote that into a planning document. SafeBath has no Spanish pages. I conflated two things to fill a gap that should have been a question.

The correct behavior would have been: "Just to confirm, you mean Spanish pages on GlobalHighLevel? SafeBath does not have Spanish content. Or did I miss something?"

### Rule 2: Ask one question at a time. Same for plans.

Stop dumping multi-question lists at the end of responses. Bill wants to chat through decisions one at a time, not be handed a 5-question survey.

Same for plans: walk through one section at a time so we can discuss before the next section. Do not dump the full plan as one big message.

Recent violation (2026-04-14): I ended a planning message with FIVE questions all at once. Bill said "never list all your questions out, ask one at a time."

## How to apply

When uncertain about a fact:
- Stop. Do not fill the gap.
- Ask one specific question.
- Wait for the answer.
- Then proceed.

When walking through a plan:
- Present ONE section or component
- Ask what Bill thinks
- Discuss
- Move to the next section
- Repeat

When ending a response:
- ONE question, ONE next step.
- Not a list of options unless Bill explicitly asks for options.
- ALWAYS include my own recommended answer with reasoning. Bill explicitly said "i always want your siggestion" (2026-04-14). Do not just present options and ask him to pick. Pick first, explain why, then let him agree or push back.

## Why

Multi-question dumps overwhelm. Bill wants conversation, not interrogation. Lists of questions force him to context-switch through five different topics in his head before answering anything. One at a time means he can actually engage with each decision properly.

Same for plans. A 2,000 word plan with 7 sections means Bill has to read it all to know if section 1 was right. If section 1 is wrong, the rest of the plan is wasted writing. One section at a time means we catch errors early.
